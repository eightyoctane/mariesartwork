Burney Falls, California

 

What an adventure this trip was! I flew to San Jose and then got into Gary's 1948 Bonanza to fly to Redding, California to paint at Burney Falls. We camped at the Burney Falls State Park. The next day, I gathered my paints and hiked down to the falls and set up on the edge of the path headed down to the bottom of the falls. You could hear the roar of the falls at the trail head!

Although Burney Falls is not the highest or largest waterfall in the state, it is possibly one of the most beautiful falls. Burney Falls originates from the park's underground springs and flows to Lake Britton, getting larger along the way to the majestic falls. Water from other springs join the falls to create a mist-filled basin.

 The park's landscape was created by volcanic activity as well as erosion from weather and streams. This volcanic region is surrounded by mountain peaks and is covered by black volcanic rock, or basalt. Created over a million years ago, the layered, porous basalt retains rainwater and melted snow, which forms a large underground reservoir.

Within the park, the water emerges as springs at and above Burney Falls, where it flows at 100 million gallons every day.

I was able to paint for hours as the mist of the falls fell across my face. I wanted the viewer to fell like they were right at the bottom of the falls and looking up at the massive roar of water spilling from the falls.
